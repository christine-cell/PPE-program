V
Voice & Variety
Keep your brain guessing — and your voice strong
+

Voice and Variety are more important than many people realize — and they come up again and again in the testimonials we hear from program participants. Without Variety, you have Monotony. Doing the same exercises day in and day out is, simply put, boring!

"If it doesn't challenge you, it won't change you."

Variety keeps your brain guessing and your body challenged. Students often share that they love showing up to every class because it's always different — they truly never know what to expect. Variety also needs to include cognitive exercises alongside physical exercise. These two elements work hand in hand to create the best possible exercise experience for someone living with Parkinson's.

With Parkinson's, your Voice can be affected — though not everyone will experience this. Your voice may become quieter, slower, or harder to understand. The more you use it, the stronger it will be. It's very important to choose an exercise class that incorporates voice exercises.

💡 Important Reminder Because your voice box is made up of muscles, you can strain it. Never skip your vocal warm-up and jump straight into loud exercises. Warm up first — then use the loudest voice you can. Voice exercises are covered in depth in Module #9.

Your voice box is a muscle too. Keep it in good shape, just as much as the rest of your body.

I
Intensity
Power and output — not just aerobic effort
+

High intensity includes aerobic exercise, but it is also about the power and output you bring to every movement. When getting off the couch, most of us naturally use adaptations — pushing off with our hands, using the armrest, or leaning on someone else. These compensatory strategies reduce the effort demanded from your core muscles.

What you may be missing is effort in the POWER and INTENSITY departments — the momentum-building effort that helps your body relearn how to move with authority.

Intense exercise — especially non-contact boxing — has been shown to reverse, reduce, or even delay the symptoms of Parkinson's disease.

G
Grand Movements
Big, bold, whole-body movements
+

"Grand Movements" is a way of saying large amplitude, whole-body movements. It is really important to emphasize every movement and make it as big and proud as possible. This is a key factor in helping your body return to normal movement patterns.

When demonstrating a big walking gait, both arms swing through their fullest range and each step is as long as possible. It may look exaggerated — but for someone with Parkinson's, copying that movement may feel completely natural.

The primary goal is simple: move big to move normally. What looks like an exaggeration for a healthy person is often exactly the right level of movement for someone with Parkinson's.

O
Overall Rotation
Rotation, rotation, rotation — your spine needs it
+

ROTATION. ROTATION. ROTATION. We all need more of it — starting with the mid-back, or thoracic region. As we age, it's common to develop a slight forward hunch in this area. For people with Parkinson's, a very rigid core is especially common.

The thoracic region is the back portion of your core. Any exercise program you follow needs to include rotation movements — because the longer you wait, the harder it becomes to get rotation back.

Rotation in your mid-back also relieves strain on your lower back and shoulders — the two most common areas of pain and injury seen in clients.
🔄 Try This Right Now Stand tall and raise both hands toward the ceiling. Now hunch forward and try again. Notice how much higher your arms go when you're upright? That's why posture and rotation matter — restricted movement leads to compensation and potential injury.

When one part of the spine stops rotating properly, other parts try to compensate. Your lower back is not designed to rotate — forcing it to do so sets the stage for injury. Keeping your thoracic region mobile protects your entire spine.

R
Release
Myofascial release — freeing the body from the inside out
+

The R in VIGOR stands for Release — specifically, Myofascial Release. What is fascia? It is the network of connective tissue that stabilizes, encloses, and separates your muscles and organs. Think of the white connective tissue you see in a piece of steak — that is fascia, and it covers your entire body.

If your fascia is restricted, your muscles cannot function at their best. Releasing tight fascia improves movement, relieves tension, and reduces overall tightness throughout the body.

Stretching must be a consistent part of your daily routine — whether it is built into your exercise class or done at home. Massage balls are excellent tools for loosening the areas that most affect alignment and posture: the back, chest, feet, and shoulders.

🏀 Recommended Tools Yoga Tune-Up Balls and Alpha Balls are highly recommended. If these feel too firm, softer balls work just as well — the key is consistency, not intensity.

You will be shown three favourite releases that you can try today. Find a comfortable space where you can get down to the ground and relax — notice the difference before and after.
