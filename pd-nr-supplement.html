Introduction

What Is NR — and Why Does It Matter for Parkinson's?

Nicotinamide Riboside (NR) is a naturally occurring form of Vitamin B3 that your body uses as a building block to produce NAD+ — nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide. NAD+ is one of the most important molecules in every cell of your body. It powers energy metabolism, repairs damaged DNA, and keeps your mitochondria — the tiny power plants inside your cells — functioning at their best.

Here is the challenge: as we age, our NAD+ levels decline significantly. This drop has been directly linked to age-related diseases including Alzheimer's and Parkinson's disease. The exciting possibility with NR is that it offers a practical, safe way to replenish NAD+ levels through supplementation — and this is exactly what researchers have been testing.

💡 The Core Idea: Mitochondrial dysfunction, neuroinflammation, and poor cellular energy are all implicated in Parkinson's disease. NAD+ helps address all three. NR is a well-tolerated, evidence-backed way to raise your body's NAD+ levels — and early clinical results in PD patients are genuinely promising.

How It Works

What NAD+ Does in the Body

Understanding why NR matters starts with understanding what NAD+ does — and why its decline is such a problem as we age.

01

⚡ Powers Cellular Energy

NAD+ is essential for mitochondria to convert food into usable energy. Low levels mean less energy — and for people with PD, already-stressed mitochondria struggle even more.

02

🛠 Repairs DNA

NAD+ is a key cofactor for enzymes that repair damaged DNA. Neurons are especially vulnerable to DNA damage, making this function critically important in neurodegeneration.

03

🛡 Reduces Inflammation

NAD+ supports anti-inflammatory pathways. In the NADPARK trial, NR supplementation measurably reduced inflammatory cytokines in both blood and cerebrospinal fluid.

04

🧹 Supports Cell Cleanup

NAD+ is involved in lysosomal and proteasomal function — the systems your cells use to clear out damaged proteins, including the alpha-synuclein implicated in Parkinson's.

🔬 What the Research Says — NR in Parkinson's Disease

Cell Metabolism · NADPARK Study · 2022
The NADPARK Study: A Randomized Phase I Trial of NR Supplementation in Parkinson's Disease

This landmark double-blind Phase I trial — led by Norwegian researchers at Haukeland University Hospital — gave 30 newly diagnosed, treatment-naive PD patients 1,000 mg of NR or placebo daily for 30 days. NR was well-tolerated and produced a significant increase in cerebral NAD+ levels. Patients whose brain NAD+ increased showed measurable changes in cerebral metabolism on PET imaging, associated with mild clinical improvement in motor scores (MDS-UPDRS). NR also increased activity of genes related to mitochondrial, lysosomal and proteasomal function, and reduced inflammatory cytokines in blood and cerebrospinal fluid.

📌 Selected among Top 10 most influential studies in Cell Metabolism 2022
Nature Communications · NR-SAFE Trial · 2023
NR-SAFE: High-Dose NR in Parkinson's Disease — Safety Confirmed

A follow-up Phase I trial tested higher doses (3,000 mg/day) in 20 PD patients. High-dose NR was found to be safe and produced a pronounced augmentation of the NAD metabolome — including up to a 5-fold increase in blood NAD+ levels. NR recipients also showed clinical improvement in total MDS-UPDRS scores. The authors concluded that Phase II trials extending to 3,000 mg/day are supported. A larger trial, the NOPARK study, is now recruiting 400 participants for a one-year investigation.

📌 5× increase in blood NAD+ levels — safe at 3,000 mg/day
"NR augmented the NAD metabolome and induced transcriptional upregulation of processes related to mitochondrial, lysosomal, and proteasomal function in blood cells and/or skeletal muscle. Furthermore, NR decreased the levels of inflammatory cytokines in serum and cerebrospinal fluid. Our findings nominate NR as a potential neuroprotective therapy for PD, warranting further investigation in larger trials."

— Brakedal et al., Cell Metabolism, 2022 · Christine's plain-language translation: It definitely made a meaningful difference in terms of energy function in skeletal muscle and blood — and reduced brain inflammation too.

Dosage & Practical Guidance

How to Take NR

NR has an excellent safety profile and is well-tolerated at doses tested in clinical trials up to 3,000 mg per day. That said, Christine always recommends starting low and building up — and working with your healthcare provider, particularly given the complexity of PD medications.

💊 Clinical trial dose: 1,000 mg/day
📈 Research-supported up to 3,000 mg/day
🌅 Take in the morning for energy support
👨‍⚕️ Always discuss with your doctor first

🛒 What to Look For: Choose a reputable brand with third-party testing. NR is sold under brand names including Tru Niagen® (ChromaDex) and Basis® (Elysium). Look for products that specify nicotinamide riboside chloride (NR-Cl), which is the form used in clinical trials.

NR is water-soluble and does not accumulate in the body — excess is eliminated naturally. It is generally taken once daily in the morning, as it supports cellular energy and may be mildly stimulating for some people.
