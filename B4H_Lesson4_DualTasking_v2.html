Dual tasking for balance — progressive challenges that work your mind and body at the same time, just like real life does.

Why dual tasking matters for Parkinson's

In real life, you never just walk. You walk and talk. You stand and search for something on a shelf. You step over a curb and answer a question.

For people living with Parkinson's, splitting attention between a physical task and a mental task is one of the biggest contributors to freezing and falls. The brain has to work harder to manage movement that used to be automatic.

The good news: dual tasking is trainable. The more you practice it, the better your brain gets at managing both — and the safer and more confident you feel in everyday life.

Before you start: Position yourself standing beside your chair with one hand resting on the back for support. Do your balance exercise while completing each challenge on screen. If you need to sit at any point — sit. Safety first, challenge second.

Level 1 — Gentle Start

Seated or standing with both hands on the chair. Simple cognitive tasks with easy physical movement.

Physical task
Sit to Stand
Rise from your chair slowly and lower back down. 10 repetitions. Use the armrests if needed.
Add this while you move
Count backwards from 20
Say the numbers out loud as you stand and sit. 20… 19… 18… If you lose count, just restart — that's the whole point.
Physical task
Weight Shift Side to Side
Standing with hands on chair, slowly shift your weight to your right foot, then left. Alternate for 20 shifts.
Add this while you move
Name the months
Say each month out loud as you shift — one month per shift. January, February… see if you get through all 12.

Level 2 — Building Up

Standing with one hand lightly on the chair. More demanding cognitive tasks added.

Physical task
Side Stepping
Lift your knee up and step over an imaginary obstacle. 10 reps each side, one hand lightly on chair.
Add this while you move
Name animals A to Z
One animal per step. Antelope, Bear, Cat, Dog… Say them out loud. It's harder than it sounds by the time you reach M.
Physical task
Hip Flexion
Standing tall, lift one knee to hip height and lower slowly. Alternate legs for 20 reps total.
Add this while you move
Alternate colours
Red, Blue, Red, Blue… or try Red, Blue, Green cycling. One colour per knee lift. Change the pattern when it gets easy.

Level 3 — Full Challenge

Standing with fingertips barely touching the chair. Demanding cognitive tasks that require real concentration.

Physical task
Wall Squats
Back against the wall, feet shoulder-width apart. Slide down slowly. Hold for 10 seconds, return to standing. 5 reps.
Add this while you move
Subtract 7 from 100
100, 93, 86, 79… Say each answer out loud as you hold the squat. One number per second. Don't stop moving.
Physical task
Stepping Back
Facing your chair with hands on the seat, take a big step backward with one leg and return. 10 each side.
Add this while you move
Tell a story
Describe your morning out loud while stepping — what you had for breakfast, what you saw, who you spoke to. Keep talking, keep moving.

Christine's coaching tips for dual tasking

  • If you lose track of the mental task, don't stop moving. Keep your body going and restart the mental task. That moment of recovery is actually great training.
  • It's normal to feel frustrated. That frustration means your brain is working. Stick with it.
  • Start at Level 1 even if it feels easy. The goal is quality of movement, not proving you can do Level 3.
  • Do these challenges 3–4 times per week for best results. Progress happens through repetition, not intensity.
  • Laugh when it goes wrong. Seriously — the people in my classes who laugh the most also tend to progress the fastest.
