Introduction

Why Magnesium Matters — Especially for Parkinson's

Magnesium is one of the most critical minerals in the human body — yet it's also one of the most chronically under-consumed. Unlike many supplements, magnesium cannot be manufactured by the body; it must come entirely from food or supplementation. And in today's world of refined grains and depleted soils, most people simply aren't getting enough.

For people living with Parkinson's disease, the stakes are even higher. Magnesium plays direct roles in protecting neurons, supporting the production of dopamine, calming overactive nerve cells, and reducing the inflammation and oxidative stress that drive PD progression. It also supports sleep, mood, energy, and muscle function — areas where PD already creates significant challenges.

💡 Christine's Note: You can definitely work on increasing your magnesium intake through food — but it's challenging to stay consistent enough or get therapeutic amounts that way. A high-quality supplement is the practical solution. This is one supplement Christine comes back to again and again with her Parkinson's clients, and the results speak for themselves.

Benefits in Depth

What Magnesium Does for You

Magnesium has several specific mechanisms that make it particularly valuable for people living with Parkinson's disease. These aren't just general health benefits — they directly target the processes that drive PD progression.

🛡️

Neuroprotection

Reduces neuronal excitability and lowers inflammation and oxidative stress — two of the key drivers of neuron damage in Parkinson's disease.

🔬

Dopamine Support

Magnesium enhances dopamine production and supports the enzymes needed for its synthesis — directly addressing the central deficiency in PD.

💊

L-DOPA Optimisation

Helps optimize the effectiveness of levodopa therapy — the primary medication for PD — making your medication work harder for you.

🧲

NMDA Receptor Block

Acts as a natural NMDA receptor blocker, reducing excitotoxicity — the overactivation of nerve cells that contributes to neuron death in PD.

Dr. Mary Claire Haver, a leading OB/GYN and women's health expert, highlights magnesium as one of the most important minerals for women as they age — particularly through perimenopause and menopause.

🌡️

Menopause Symptoms

Helps alleviate hot flashes, night sweats, and mood swings by calming the nervous system and reducing inflammation associated with hormonal shifts.

⚖️

Hormone Balance

Essential for balancing estrogen and progesterone, and supports adrenal health — which directly affects how the body manages hormonal stress.

😴

Sleep & Melatonin

Enhances melatonin production and calms the nervous system, directly improving sleep quality — which often deteriorates significantly during menopause.

🦴

Bone Strength

Works alongside calcium and Vitamin D to maintain bone density, counteracting the increased osteoporosis risk that comes with estrogen decline in menopause.

😊

Mood & Serotonin

Supports serotonin production and helps regulate cortisol levels, reducing anxiety and depression that are common during hormonal transitions.

🩸

PMS & Period Relief

For women still menstruating, magnesium relaxes muscles and regulates neurotransmitters, significantly easing bloating, cramps, and mood swings.

Beyond its specific roles in PD and hormonal health, magnesium is foundational to dozens of everyday functions that affect how you feel, move, and think every single day.

⚡

Energy Production

Magnesium is required to produce ATP — the molecule that powers every cell in your body. Low magnesium = low cellular energy at the most fundamental level.

🩸

Blood Sugar Balance

Regulates glucose, lipid, and protein metabolism, supporting insulin sensitivity and helping keep blood sugar stable throughout the day.

💪

Muscle Function

Maintains nerve and muscle function by regulating calcium — the mineral responsible for muscle contractions. Without enough magnesium, muscles stay "switched on."

🔥

Anti-inflammatory

Plays a direct role in reducing systemic inflammation and oxidative stress — both of which accelerate aging, neurodegeneration, and disease progression.

Which Form Is Right for You?

Not All Magnesium Is Equal

The form of magnesium you choose makes a significant difference to how well it's absorbed and what it's best suited for. Here are the three most clinically relevant forms for PPE clients.

⭐ Christine's #1 Pick
💚

Magnesium Glycinate

Best for: Sleep · Stress · Anxiety · PD

The gentlest form on the stomach and the most bioavailable. Bound to glycine (a calming amino acid), it's exceptional for sleep quality, stress relief, and nerve calming. This is Christine's top recommendation — she sells it in liquid form (pomegranate flavour, Genestra brand) and it's the most popular supplement among her clients.

🍋

Magnesium Citrate

Best for: Constipation · Gut health

Highly absorbable and has a gentle laxative effect — making it an excellent choice for the constipation that is one of the most common non-motor symptoms of Parkinson's disease. Also a good general supplementation option when cost is a consideration.

🍎

Magnesium Malate

Best for: Energy · Muscle pain · Fatigue

Bound to malic acid, which plays a key role in energy production. An excellent choice for people experiencing fatigue, muscle pain, or post-exertional malaise. Particularly relevant for PD clients struggling with the energy demands of daily life and exercise.

👨 Men: 420 mg / day (RDI)
👩 Women: 320 mg / day (RDI)
🌙 Take glycinate at night for best sleep benefit
⚠️ Discuss dose with your doctor first

🔬 Testing Your Levels: Don't guess — test! Ask your doctor for an RBC magnesium test (red blood cell magnesium), which gives a much better picture of your true magnesium status than a standard serum test. Hair testing can also indicate long-term deficiencies, particularly in neurological conditions.
