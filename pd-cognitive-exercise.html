Why It Matters

Parkinson's Isn't Just a Movement Disorder

When most people think of Parkinson's disease, they picture tremors, stiffness, and slowness of movement. But research has firmly established that PD is much more than that. Cognitive changes — including difficulties with memory, attention, processing speed, executive function (planning and problem-solving), and visuospatial skills — are among the most common and impactful non-motor symptoms of the disease.

🔬 A landmark 2021 review in Nature Reviews Disease Primers (Aarsland et al.) confirmed that cognitive impairment in PD is now recognised as a defined clinical entity — and one that profoundly affects quality of life, independence, and caregiver burden.

Up to 75% of people living with Parkinson's will develop some degree of cognitive impairment over the course of their disease. Yet cognitive health is one of the areas where proactive, non-pharmacological interventions like cognitive training can genuinely make a difference — and the evidence to support this is growing fast.

The good news? The brain has a remarkable ability to adapt, rewire, and build new connections — a phenomenon called neuroplasticity. And just like physical exercise keeps your muscles strong, cognitive exercise keeps your brain resilient. You can actually train it.

🔬 Neuroplasticity — Your Brain Can Change

Neuroplasticity is the brain's lifelong capacity to reorganise itself by forming new neural connections. This is not just a theory — it's measurable. Cognitive training studies in PD have used MRI imaging to show actual structural changes in white matter microstructure after just 8 weeks of training (Vriend et al., 2021, Neurotherapeutics).

A key player in this process is Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF) — essentially your brain's own fertiliser. Exercise and cognitive stimulation both increase BDNF levels, which promotes the survival and growth of neurons, strengthens synaptic connections, and has been shown to support hippocampal neurogenesis — the birth of brand new brain cells.

The most exciting finding from dual-task research (doing a physical and mental task at the same time) is the "guided plasticity facilitation hypothesis" — motor exercise opens the door for neuroplasticity, and cognitive stimulation guides where that plasticity goes. Together, they are more powerful than either alone.

The Evidence

What the Research Actually Shows

This is no longer fringe science. Multiple high-quality randomised controlled trials and large systematic reviews now support cognitive training as a meaningful intervention for people with Parkinson's disease.

Parkinsonism & Related Disorders · 2022 · Amsterdam UMC
8-Week Online Cognitive Training — Double-Blind RCT

van Balkom and colleagues at Amsterdam's Vrije Universiteit conducted a rigorous double-blind randomised controlled trial of 8 weeks of online cognitive training in people with PD. The trial showed measurable improvements in cognitive performance and, notably, white matter microstructure changes on MRI — meaning the brain physically changed. A follow-up 2023 study confirmed that while booster sessions are needed to sustain effects long-term, participants who completed training showed significantly fewer depressive symptoms at follow-up.

📌 Key finding: Brain structure measurably changed after 8 weeks
Ageing Research Reviews · 2022 · Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis
Computerised Cognitive Training in PD — 14 RCTs

Gavelin and colleagues conducted a comprehensive systematic review and meta-analysis following PRISMA 2020 guidelines, covering 14 high-quality randomised controlled trials of computerised cognitive training in Parkinson's disease. The review found consistent positive effects across multiple cognitive domains, with the most robust evidence for attention, processing speed, and executive function. Critically, improvements were maintained for up to 18 months in studies that included follow-up booster sessions.

📌 Key finding: Benefits maintained up to 18 months with booster sessions
Brain Sciences · 2023 · Meta-Analysis · West China Hospital, Sichuan University
Motor-Cognitive Dual-Task Training — Impact on Brain & Body

This meta-analysis synthesised evidence from multiple RCTs on dual-task training — where participants perform a physical and cognitive task simultaneously (e.g., walking while counting backwards). The research showed dual-task training improved both physical and cognitive outcomes in PD, outperforming either type of training alone. The proposed mechanism is the "guided plasticity facilitation hypothesis": physical exercise stimulates BDNF and opens the brain for neuroplasticity, while the cognitive component guides where those new connections form.

📌 Key finding: Dual-task training superior to either physical or cognitive training alone
Archives of Physical Medicine & Rehabilitation · 2023
Dual-Task Training Meta-Analysis — Gait, Attention & Cognition

Wong, Cheng, Yang and colleagues published a meta-analysis with moderate-quality evidence demonstrating that dual-task training significantly improved dual-task gait speed, step and stride length, and measures of attention in people with PD — compared to usual care or conventional physical training alone. This is particularly important because dual-task performance (e.g., walking while talking) is a major determinant of fall risk and independence in PD.

📌 Key finding: Dual-task gait and attention both improved with cognitive-motor training
Brain Sciences / MDPI Systematic Review · 2025 · 23 RCTs
Comprehensive Identification of Cognitive Training for PD

The most recent 2025 systematic review — covering 23 randomised controlled trials across PubMed, PsycINFO, and Web of Science — found strong evidence across a range of cognitive training modalities, including exergames, virtual reality, executive function training, multidomain programmes, and dual-task interventions. The authors highlight that cognitive impairment in PD "might lead to an increased risk of developing dementia," making early and sustained cognitive training a priority.

📌 Key finding: Multiple modalities effective — start early, be consistent
Frontiers in Neuroscience · 2025
Physical Activity, Neuroplasticity & Cognitive Training — Comprehensive Review

This sweeping 2025 Frontiers review confirmed that the combination of physical activity and cognitive training produces additive neuroplastic benefits far beyond either alone. Specifically for PD, virtual reality-based exercise systems with cognitive components improved motor function by 35% compared to conventional physiotherapy. The review also highlights that BDNF — elevated by both physical and cognitive exercise — is a central mediator of these brain-changing effects.

📌 Key finding: Combined physical + cognitive training = most powerful approach

💡 The Power of Dual-Tasking

One of the most exciting insights from recent research is the power of doing two things at once — a cognitive task and a physical task simultaneously. This directly targets a known challenge in PD: the brain's reduced capacity to divide attention between competing tasks, which is why walking and talking can feel so much harder than it used to.

Try it yourself today: count backwards from 100 by 3s while walking. Or recite the alphabet while doing gentle exercises. These aren't party tricks — they're evidence-based interventions that train exactly the cognitive-motor pathways that Parkinson's challenges.
